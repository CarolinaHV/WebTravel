NEW YORK HISTORY

The history of New York is only known from the 16th century when Manhattan was occupied by the Iroquois and Algonquin Indians. The negotiations that led to the current configuration with five boroughs: Manhattan, Queens, Brooklin, Bronx and Staten Island date back to the 19th century and to the first half of the 20th century. In the beginning, the crime rate was very high due to various conflicts, but despite this, it managed to gain progressively greater importance, to the point of constituting today the largest city in the United States and one of the most important and dynamic metropolises in the world. world. This is its chronology since Giovanni da Verrazzano discovered it in 1524.

  • 1524: The first explorer to glimpse the island of Manhattan in 1524 was the navigator Giovanni da Verrazzano at the service of King Francis I of France, when he was looking for a route linking Europe with Asia.
  • 1609: The second to explore the bay was the British Henry Hudson, at the service of the Dutch East India Company, explores the entire area going up the river that today bears his name to Albany. Hudson arrived in New York Bay on September 12 with the Half Moon ship.
  • 1614: The Dutch were the first Europeans to settle in the area and in 1614 they built Fort Nassau, nowadays Albany.
  • 1625: The Dutch East India Company is established in Manhattan and creates the first European colony under the name of Nieuw Amsterdam.
  • 1626: Governor of the Dutch colony Peter Minuit buys the island of Manhattan from the Indians for an equivalent of $ 24, the Dutch colony officially emerged and specialized in the fur trade with the Indians.
  • 1647: Peter Stuyvesant is elected governor of the Dutch colony and undertakes a set of construction projects that included bridges, schools, piers.
  • 1653: On February 2, Nieuw Amsterdam is founded as a city. A wall is built to protect itself from indigenous attacks on what is known as Wall Street.
  • 1664: On September 8, the English under Colonel Nicholls conquer Nieuw Amsterdam and rename the city as New York in honor of the Duke of York.
  • 1754: With the English New York gained importance and prosperity as a commercial port and Columbia University is founded.
  • 1775: The United States War of Independence begins, New York played an important role being the scene of several battles.
  • 1776: On July 4, the Congress, meeting in Philadelphia, approved the Declaration of Independence - written by Thomas Jefferson - of thirteen independent colonies forming the United States.
  • 1783: On September 3, the end of the war is signed in the Treaty of Paris and the independence of the United States is recognized. The English are expelled and on November 25 General Washington is received triumphantly in New York.
  • 1789: Congress meets in New York and George Washintong is named president, and it was the federal capital for a year until he moved to Washington D.C..
  • 1792: On May 17, 24 stock players founded the first stock market by gathering under a tree on Wall Street.
  • 1797: the New York state government settled in Albany. Since then, only its economic role explained the growth of New York.
  • 1811:New York Mayor Witt Clinton designs the current grid-like city layout of 12 avenues and 155 streets.
  • 1820: New York became the center of Northern abolitionist activism.
  • 1825: The Erie Canal that connects the Hudson River in Albany with the Great Lakes opens. New York, being a forced passage, generates great benefits.
  • 1835: Immigration and development transformed the city, New York became the largest city in the United States.
  • 1837: Samuel Morse presents his invention in New York: the telegraph.
  • 1851: On September 18, the first issue of the New York Times goes out.
  • 1857: The year of the great economic depression.
  • 1858: The works of Central Park begin that would last 18 years.
  • 1863: The worker revolts that took place in the city for four days caused more than a thousand deaths.
  • 1865: Abraham Lincoln is assassinated. The body is exposed in the City Hall, the city council of the city.
  • 1869: On September 24, known as Black Friday, financial panic ensues on Wall Street due to gold price speculation.
  • 1880: Pearl Street generator starts up to supply electricity to first city subscribers.
  • 1886: On October 28, President Grover Cleveland inaugurates the Statue of Liberty.
  • 1892: The Castle Garden Immigration Reception Center is too small to receive migratory avalanches. New facility opens on Ellis Island.
  • 1895: On November 25, the first Broadway theater opens: the Olympia.
  • 1904: On December 31, it is celebrated for the first time in Times Square the end of the year.
  • 1913: The works of the world's largest train station are finished: the Grand Central Terminal.
  • 1925: On February 21, the publication New Yorker goes out.
  • 1929: On October 29, known as the "Black Tuesday" the stock market collapses.
  • 1931: On May 1, the Empire State Building opens, then the tallest building in the world.
  • 1939: The Universal Exposition is held in Flushing Meadow (Queens).
  • 1947: On June 1, John Fitzgerald Kennedy International Airport opens its doors.
  • 1950: United Nations headquarters moves to Manhattan.
  • 1973: After 7 years of construction, the World Trade Center is inaugurated. The works would not be completed until 4 years later.
  • 1980: On December 8, John Lennon is assassinated at the door of the Dakota, the apartment building where he lived.
  • 1989: The first black mayor of New York is elected: Democrat David Dinkins.
  • 1993: On February 26, 373 kilograms of explosives caused 6 deaths and more than 1,000 injuries in the first attack on the World Trade Center. Republican Rudolph Guiliani, known for cleaning the streets of crime with his motto "zero tolerance", is elected mayor of New York.
  • 1999: Two million people gather in Times Square to celebrate the end of the millennium.
  • 2001: On September 11, terrorist attacks occur in Washington and New York. The World Trade Center is totally destroyed.
  • 2003: On August 14, a power outage caused the largest blackout in history on the East Coast. New York would be in the dark from 4:10 pm for more than 20 hours.
  • 2011: Hurricane Irene arrived in New York on Sunday, August 28 in the form of a tropical storm, causing only material damage to the city. On Monday, August 29, little by little everything was returning to normal.
  • 2012: On Monday, October 29, Hurricane Sandy hit the eastern part of the United States. New York ordered the evacuation of residents in low-lying areas affecting 375,000 New Yorkers.
  • 2015: On Friday, May 29, at 9 in the morning, the new observatory of the World Trade Center opened to the public.

TOURIST PLACES

Times Square

Times Square is now considered the heart of New York. In time, it has become the symbol of this vibrant metropolis. With its neon lights and billboards, Times Square is New York’s most famous landmark and is the liveliest area in the city, located at the intersection of Broadway and 7th Avenue. In Times Square you will find bars, restaurants, theatres, museums and a great atmosphere.

Fifth Avenue

The most famous street and the main artery of New York is Fifth Avenue. It connects the north and south of Manhattan.

The Big Apple’s most famous street is Fifth Avenue, also known as Millionaire’s Row. It crosses the whole of Manhattan from north to south. It is New York’s most expensive and best shopping street with the world’s most expensive retail spaces. We can compare this avenue to the Champs-Élysées in Paris or Ginza in Tokio.

Empire State Building

Constructed with 102 floors and standing 381 meters tall, the Empire State Building was the tallest building in the world from 1931 to 1972. After the destruction of the Twin Towers, it became the third tallest building in New York after the skyscrapers One World Trade Center and 432 Park Avenue. The 350 5th Avenue skyscraper has 102 floors and stands 1,454 feet (443 m) tall with its antenna spire included. The building was erected in record time, taking 410 days to complete. Despite the Great Depression, over 3,000 workers were hired for the construction in 1930, building 4 floors and a half per week.

The Empire State Building has two observation decks, the first on the 86th floor, offering 360º degree views of the city and the second on the 102nd floor. Going to the first observation deck costs US$32 and going to the second costs an extra US$20, from 8 a.m. to 2 a.m. every day.

See a Broadway show

If you want to know why Frank Sinatra called New York "The City That Never Sleeps," look no further than Times Square. The heart of the Theater District is bright no matter what time of day it is, and it's also where the world tunes in to watch the ball drop on New Year's Eve. Stop by the TKTS Discount Booth for to snag same-day theater tickets at up to 50 percent off regular prices, or you can take in outdoor theater at Shakespeare in the Park, seeing a Broadway show is great, but if you’re in New York in the summertime, nothing beats Shakespeare in the Park. It’s a free series put on by the Public Theater in an open-air venue in Central Park, and combines astonishing performances from incredible talent with a night under the stars for an unbeatable theatrical experience.

Central Park

Explore Central Park, one of the largest urban parks in the world and one of the most famous symbols of New York. Discover its main sights. 843 acres are open to visitors in America's most-visited urban park, which was designed by 1858 in by landscape architects Frederick Law Olmsted and Calvert Vaux after they beat out 32 competitors for the project. Now a National Historic Landmark, the park is full of places to explore,. starting with its zoo, Bethesda Fountain, the mall (shown here), and Woolman Rink. Even the Metropolitan Museum of Art technically falls within its confines.

Columbia University campus

Even if your college days are in the past, you can still still explore the city's only Ivy League institution. Head out on a self-guided tour or choose one of the options with a guide, including one that covers the history, architecture, and sculpture of the Morningside Heights campus. For more architectural splendor in the neighborhood, don't miss the Cathedral of Saint John the Divine.

New York Botanical Garden

The 250-acre National Historic Landmark in the Bronx hosts more than a million visitors annually. One of its top draws? The Holiday Train Show, which runs from November through January.

Lincoln Center

The Metropolitan Opera, New York Philharmonic, Chamber Music Society of Lincoln Center, and New York City Ballet all perform here. The Met Opera House, in particular, is worth visiting even without a show ticket. Its opulence is a feast for the eyes.

Museum of the City of New York

Located at the northern end of Fifth Avenue's Museum Mile, MCNY offers exhibitions on the city's art and history, including "New York at Its Core," which charts the city’s rise from a striving Dutch village to today’s 'Capital of the World.'

American Museum of Natural History

Founded in 1869, the American Museum of Natural History now has more than 32 million specimens and cultural artifacts in its collection. Stroll through the museum's 570,000-square-foot space on Central Park West, which includes 45 permanent exhibit halls including dinosaur fossils, stunning dioramas, and, in the Milstein Family Hall of Ocean Life, a 94-foot-long, 21,000-pound model of a blue whale suspended from the ceiling.

Admire the architecture and exhibitions at the Morgan Library

"There are bigger and more famous museums in Manhattan, but the Morgan Library—built in the early 1900s by McKim, Mead & White to house the library of Pierpont Morgan—is something of a hidden gem. The museum houses impressive exhibitions you won’t catch at other institutions, and its permanent collection of manuscripts and rare books is one of a kind. Stop in for a look and stay for lunch at the café."

New York's spy museum

Located in the middle of Midtown, SPYSCAPE provides an interactive deep-dive into the worlds of deception, encryption, surveillance, hacking, intelligence, cyber warfare, and special ops. One especially cool feature is the Special Ops Tunnels, laser mazes that test visitors' agility and reaction times.

Visit one of the city's cathedrals

St Patrick's Cathedral is an iconic symbol of New York. Built in honor of St Patrick, Patron Saint of Ireland, it was established in a Neo-Gothic style in 1858.

The Cathedral of St. Patrick is the largest Neo-Gothic-style Roman Catholic cathedral in the United States. It was dedicated to the Patron Saint of Ireland. Although the construction of the cathedral began in 1858, works were stopped during the American Civil War and the temple wasn’t completed until 1879.

The Cathedral of Saint John the Divine is The most monumental Anglican Cathedral of the world is located in New York City. Designed in a Gothic-style, St John the Divine Cathedral is worth discovering It currently serves as the seat of the Archbishop of the Episcopal Diocese of New York.

Grand Central Terminal

Whether you head downstairs for stew at the Grand Central Oyster Bar, upstairs for a cocktail at the Campbell or a match at the Vanderbilt Tennis Club, or are just passing through to catch a train, the station, completed in 1913, is an architectural marvel.

Study (or pretend to) at the New York Public Library

Walk up the steps of the Beaux-Arts Stephen A. Schwarzman Building of the New York Public Library and past the Library Lions to discover one of the city's best public spaces. Tours are available, and you should be sure not to miss the Rose Main Reading Room; it's roughly the length of two city blocks and features 52-foot-tall ceilings with murals of skies and clouds.

Go on a culinary tour of Chelsea Market

This food market, which features shops and stalls that specialize in everything from hummus (Dizengoff) and halvah (Seed + Mill) to spices, fish, matcha, tacos, and kitchen supplies, was way ahead of its time in bringing together disparate vendors under one roof, and it continues to be a great way to spend a weekend morning (it does get really busy in the afternoons).

High Line Elevated Park

Inaugurated in 2009, is one of the loveliest green-spaces in New York City. It was built on an old railway line. High Line is an urban park built on an elevated section of the City’s disused Central Railroad. The elevated platform stopped being used in 1980. Twenty-three years later several ideas of possible usage were discussed and it was decided that it would be transformed into a park. The urban park project was finished in 2014.

High Line Park is 1.45 mile-long (2.33 km) and runs from Gansevoort Street to 34th Street in Lower West Side of Manhattan. This greenway is divided in three sections and has several accesses all along the park.

High Line has benches all along its path to sit and enjoy the views or lie and sunbathe, and has become a favourite among New Yorkers during the summer months. The park was designed by James Corner Field Operations; and the first section was opened on 9 June, 2009.

Tesla's store

Tesla debuted its newest showroom in the heart of the Meatpacking District in 2017. The 10,900-square-foot space offers an up-close-and-personal look at the automaker's electric vehicles, including the Model S, Model X, and the Model 3.

Check out the One World Trade Center Observatory.

The new World Trade Center’s main building includes three different observation decks in which you can contemplate the stunning views of New York City and feel the weight of its historical and emotional past. . On a clear day, you can see up to 50 miles from the top of the 1,776-foot-tall skyscraper. It stands 104 floors high and it is currently open to the public. If you wish to visit this landmark, you must pre-book tickets.

National September 11 Memorial & Museum

Opened in May 2014, 13 years after the tragic terrorist attacks that changed the world, The 9/11 Memorial (shown here) includes two massive pools that cover the original footprints of the Twin Towers, with nearly 3,000 names of the men, women, and children killed in the attacks inscribed into bronze walls surrounding the pools. Downstairs, the 9/11 Memorial Museum displays artifacts linked to the events of 9/11 and tells the stories of the victims. To discover the museum will take visitors minimum 2 hours, but one could spend a whole day in the Memorial Museum and not see everything there is to see.

Walk over the Brooklyn Bridge

The Brooklyn Bridge is one of New York's main historic symbols. It is also a fundamental part of the city's infraestructure, as it crossed the East River connecting Manhattan and Brooklyn. The Brooklyn Bridge became the first steel-wire suspension bridge to be constructed. It was opened in 1883 after 13 years in construction, resulting in the death of 27 people. It has a span of 1,595.5 feet (486.3 m), and a distance of 500 m between its two towers., offers a quintessential New York experience for walkers and bikers. It also features some of the best views of the city's skyline.

the Statue of Liberty on Ellis Island

New York’s most famous symbol, the Statue of Liberty, also known as Liberty Enlightening the World, is found on Liberty Island. New York’s most famous symbol, the Statue of Liberty, also known as Liberty Enlightening the World, is found on Liberty Island. This colossal monument of 151 feet 1 inch from base to torch, located on an island in Upper New York Bay, can only be visited by ferry. To get to the boat visitors have to head to the dock in Battery Park in Downtown Manhattan.

On the 4 of July, 2009, coinciding with Independence Day of the United States (a very important date for the Statue), the access to the pedestal was reopened. It had previously been closed since the 9/11 attacks.

To go up to the pedestal you will have to buy the tickets on the Internet weeks in advance, otherwise it’s impossible to visit the statue's observation deck, since only 30 people can enjoy the views per hour.

Rockefeller Center

In the heart of Manhattan is located the Rockefeller Center, an enormous complex with various Art-Deco buildings, Rockefeller Center is a 19 building complex located in the heart of Manhattan, between Fifth and Sixth Avenue. Its most important skyscraper is now called Comcast building (formerly the GE Building), built in 1930 with 70 floors and standing 640-foot (200 m)-tall.

Although the architecture of the complex is fascinating, Rockefeller Center especially stands out for its history and atmosphere.

The complex has a series of stores and restaurants in its underground pedestrian passages (The Concourse) and you will find some of New York’s best shops at the entrance of Fifth Avenue (Channel Gardens). Commissioned by the Rockefeller family, it was built by John D. Rockefeller Jr. in 1937. The idea was to transform the area into the second business district of New York, after Wall Street.

During Christmas, Rockefeller Center becomes one of the liveliest and most popular parts of the city, with its famous Christmas tree and ice-skating ring. Top of the Rock on the 70th floor of the Comcast building is the observation-deck Top of the Rock. This viewpoint is a good alternative to the Empire State Building observation deck. Although it is less high, the views of Central Park and of the Empire State Building make it definitely worthwhile.

Wall Street

Located in Lower Manhattan, Wall Street is the main street of the Financial District of New York City. It includes famous buildings such as the New York Stock Exchange, built in the early twentieth century, even though it has a very modern aspect, and the Federal Hall, rebuilt in the late eighteenth century. Before the September 11 attacks, it was possible to visit the Stock Exchange and witness the lively atmosphere from the top level of the building. Nowadays, it is no longer open to tourists. To presence the bustling atmosphere of this area we recommend you visit Wall Street from Monday to Friday during working hours. On the weekends, the landscape is completely different.

Metropolitan Museum of Art (MET)

The Metropolitan Museum of Art, also known as “the Met”, is one of the most visited museums in New York City and is also ranked in the top ten most visited museums in the world. It welcomes over 6 million visitors a year. Discover its art collections and its masterpieces, it is not always possible to stop and see every artwork it holds as it is extremely big and would take hours. If you have little time, the most impressive galleries are the Egyptian, Greek and Roman art. We also recommend going to the second floor to see its impressive paintings.

Madame Tussauds

With over 200 figures and located on Times Square, New York’s Madame Tussauds is one of the most famous wax museums in the world. The Opening Night Party, which is a party-decorated room where you’ll be able to see and take photos of the world’s most famous celebrities, is one of the best sections of the museum.

After visiting the Opening Night Party, you’ll walk through other themed rooms, where you’ll bump into different U.S. Presidents, singers, sportsmen and women, leaders, militaries and other celebrities. You’ll also walk into the White House’s yellow oval room to meet Obama and we recommend taking a photo as it is extremely realistic!.

If you’re not easily scared, then we dare you to go to the "Scream attraction", where the wax figures are mixed with real actors who will go to great lengths to frighten the visitors.

Museum of Modern Art (MoMA)

The MoMA is one of the largest museums of modernist and contemporary art and; therefore, it will take you at least two hours to see the whole museum (at a quick pace). If you don’t have enough time or if modern art isn’t what most interests you, then we recommend you visit at least the 4th and 5th floor, where you will find some of history’s most famous paintings.

The collection currently holds almost 200,000 works of art. Considered by many to have the best collections of modern art, the MoMA holds masterpieces such as The Starry Night by Vincent Van Gogh, Les Demoiselles d’Avignon by Picasso, The Persistence of Memory by Salvador Dalí and Claude Monet’s Water Lilies.

In Manhatta you will eat with incredible views of Lower Manhattan. The restaurant is located on the 60th floor, a few minutes walk from Wall Street and the 9/11 Memorial. JG Melon

Upper East Side preppies have gathered faithfully at J.G. Melon since 1972, but its melon kitsch décor makes this local landmark look like it opened back in 1932. Under a pressed tin-ceiling and dim yellowed lighting, ruddy-cheeked young men drink Bloody Marys next to casually attired older couples munching salads out of wooden bowls.

Space is tight — the narrow, old-time bar and kitchen take up most of the front room while patrons sit elbow-to-elbow in the back — but this doesn’t dissuade couples in suits from grabbing an after-work drink, though you won’t want to impart any secrets in this jovial, noisy environment.

The resolutely American menu is painted onto wooden boards and served daily from lunch until the wee-morning hours, but the only thing anyone really orders are the thick, juicy hamburgers and cheeseburgers, the house’s signature dish. Melon’s has expanded in recent years with locations in Greenwich Village and on the Upper West Side, but this original location is by far the most charming of the bunch.

La Birreria

Nestled on the 14th floor, above the Italian megastore Eataly, you find this rooftop bar and restaurant called La Birreria. As the name suggest, the setting is like a large beer garden.

The terrace is a bit closed off, so while the view is nice over the close by Flatiron building and the Empire State Building, it is not the best in the city. But instead there is a great atmosphere to make up for it.

There is an exiting concept with new pop-up restaurants at the rooftop every now and then, however always with an Italian base. The 2020 season will see the roof turn into Serra, an Italian countryside restaurant, available also during the colder months as Serra Alpina.

Clinton St Baking Co.

Let's take a test: when you hear brunch, are the first things that come to mind are mountains of soft pancakes? For us, the blueberry pancakes from this famous bakery are the best in New York. And huge! And, if you accompany them with one of their smoothies, even better. The only ‘but’: they only accept cash.

It’s a charming little establishment, family owned and operated by a husband and wife team who put that little extra love into their food to make you remember it and want to come back.



ITINERARY

If you are one of those who do not want to miss a detail and make the most of your visit, if you prefer to travel on your own, we recommend that, whenever you can, buy in advance the tickets for the attractions that you will visit, because that way you will avoid queues and you'll save a lot of time. An important suggestion is that you start your tour very early, especially if you will be there for a few days, because there is a lot to see and do in New York.

Tips:

  • Get a New York Explorer Pass tourist card
  • The metro is the fastest way to travel between the attractions of the city and save time. Buy the Metro Card, a card with unlimited metro / bus trips. The 7-day one costs $ 33
  • Climb to the Top of the Rock at sunset to finish big that first day in New York.

Midtown Manhattan

The first days are dedicated to the most iconic New York, the one you saw on your television screen a thousand and one times. We are talking about Midtown, the heart of Manhattan, where you can find emblematic places such as Times Square, Saint Patrick's Cathedral, Bryant Park, the New York Public Library, Grand Central Terminal, or skyscrapers like the Empire State Building or the Rockefeller Center (and its Top of the Rock viewpoint).

Hudson Yards, Meatpacking District, Chelsea and Broadway

The first thing you will do, after a good breakfast, is go to the new area of ​​the Hudson Yards and see The Vessel, the monument in the shape of a space basket that is driving New Yorkers crazy. Remember that you will have to request your tickets (free) in advance.

The next destination is the High Line, a curious elevated park that travels through the heights of New York on the layout of an old urban train line. At the end of the High Line and after kicking its 2.4 km, you will find yourself in the Meatpacking District, once a quite depressed area and today is one of the coolest in the entire city.

through Meatpacking and you will arrive in the Chelsea neighborhood where you have a date with its famous market: the Chelsea Market. You can have a snack at their stalls and pause before continuing to Hell’s Kitchen, an area full of bars and venues.

These districts do not have specific points to visit, but it is best to walk through them and get involved with their environment and architecture. Of course, don't miss three of Kobra's best murals (Mount Rushmore, Gandhi and Mother Teresa, and We Love NY with Einstein).

The last stop of the day is Broadway! What better than to celebrate your stay in the city that never sleeps with a musical?

West Village, Greenwich Village, Soho, Nolita, Little Italy and Chinatown

This is one of the most popular routes in New York: we will depart from Madison Square Park, where the curious Flatiron building is located. In addition to contemplating it (and of course, taking a photo session), we like it because right in front of it there is a square with public tables and chairs where we love to have breakfast (grab a bagel and a coffee in one of the hundreds of street carts).

After gaining strength, we will start going down towards the West Village, a very cute and quite neighborhood with its typical brick houses, to then go through Soho and Nolita. Do not miss a visit to Washington Square, a square with a lot of atmosphere.

The next stop will be the mythical neighborhoods of Little Italy and Chinatown, although you pass through lots of different districts, they really are next to each other and the distance will not be so close. The most interesting thing is to see how the panorama changes drastically when going from one to the other. In Chinatown the options for cheap food are almost endless, but our favorite is the Wah Fung Fast Food where for $ 4 you have super delicious bbq rice with pork.

Take the opportunity to end the day with a beer at Rooftop 93, the terrace bar of the Wyndham Garden Chinatown hotel, with stunning views of the city. There is happy hour from 5 to 8 pm and the beer costs $ 7 (try the Brooklyn Brewery). It is the rooftop bar that we enjoy most of all those who visit.

Tour of contrasts

The Contrast Tour will give you a generic view of what New York is, beyond Manhattan. Did you know that the city is made up of 5 different neighborhoods? Manhattan, Bronx, Queens, Brooklyn, and Staten Island. With the tour of contrasts you will discover corners of the Bronx, Queens and Brooklyn (the latter is a great one).

At the end of the tour you will be dropped off in the northern part of Brooklyn, so you will have time to explore it thoroughly before crossing the magnificent Brooklyn Bridge and enjoying some of the best views of the city. It is also a good area to try what for some is the best hamburger in New York:the Shake Shack chain.

Lower Manhattan + Statue of Liberty and Ellis Island

the Lower Manhattan area, focuses on the Financial District. It passes through the mythical Wall Street and its two statues (the one of the bull and the one of the girl), the New York Stock Exchange building, the Federal Hall with the statue of George Washington on its steps, the Trinity Church, the National Museum American Indian, or the pretty street of Stone Street.

Also visit the World Trade Center area, where you will see some square fountains in the places where the Twin Towers were.
