HISTORY

The city of London, founded by the Romans under the name of Londinium in the year 43, is located in the south of England and is the political and economic capital of the United Kingdom. The city center is located 60 km from the mouth of the Tamesis, the river that runs through the city. Its privileged location in the center of south west England benefited it and, for a long time, it was the most populous and wealthy region in the country.

The city does not appear until after the Roman conquest. Roman rule extended from the 1st century AD. until the 5th century, when the Empire fell. In the 3rd century, Londinium, with its port, was an important nucleus of population, with some 50,000 inhabitants.

Ruined by the Anglo-Saxon invasions of the 5th century, in the 7th century it became the capital of the small Essex kingdom and was the episcopal see.

Although it suffered from Scandinavian incursions in the 9th century, the establishment of Danish settlers in the neighborhood encouraged entrepreneurship and a desire for commerce, making it the first urban center in the country. Its wealth attracted the Scandinavian and Danish kings who besieged the city and forced it to pay tribute.

From 1067 the city had the same rights as a county and only depended on the royal authority. The Tower of London dates from this time.

In 1191 the city was constituted as a municipality (corporation) by replacing a mayor with the prime minister (portreeve). In 1215, London had the privilege of electing its mayor every year.

For a long time, England lacked fixed capital. From the thirteenth century, Westminster, a place near London, became one of the main seats of the Government. Furthermore, the rise of European trade was another stimulant to make London the capital of the kingdom.

An expanding city

During the XIV century the port of London became a center for the distribution of goods. This activity was reinforced in the 15th century by a powerful textile industry. From the 16th century to the mid-18th century, London benefited from the political centralization and expansion of maritime trade developed by the Tudors and continued by the Stuarts. During the reign of Enrique VIII the city had about 100,000 inhabitants. In the mid-seventeenth century they numbered 500,000.

In 1665, although the great urban planning plans had already begun, most of the city was enclosed within the old wall, which led to a serious plague epidemic that caused 70,000 victims. The following year, a gigantic fire destroyed four fifths of the city. The reconstruction of the city, the base of the area known today as the City, took into account urban needs, and the masterpieces of the architect Wren embellished the new London. The city became the center of English social life, with its palaces and its halls, its theaters, its cultural societies (Royal Society, 1662) and its museums (British Museum, 1753).

London's growth was fueled by the founding, in 1694, of the Bank of England.

Much of modern London belongs to the Victorian era. Until the beginning of the 19th century, the capital was reduced to the limits of the original Roman city, plus Westminster and Mayfair, surrounded by fields. Industrialization attracted an increasing number of people who filled these green spaces. This rapid expansion caused serious problems such as the cholera epidemic of 1832, or the "great pestilence" in 1858, caused by the stench of the Thames that forced the parliamentary sessions to be suspended.

Since 1750 the population went from 700,000 inhabitants to more than 4,500,000 in 1901 (6,600,000 with the suburban area). By the end of the 19th century, London had become the capital of international finance and trade.

The administrative needs of a center of so much commercial activity prompted the creation in 1888 of a new autonomous territorial unit, the county of London, governed by the "London county council". This county was divided into twenty-nine electoral units (the city and 28 metropolitan boroughs), but the progressive expansion soon spilled out of the county limits as a very rapidly developing suburban area was formed. After a stationary period, the population of the capital began to decrease at the end of the First World War and fell below 3,500,000 by 1950. In return, the suburban area has grown steadily.

In 1963 a new division of the London agglomeration was carried out, comprising the old town and 32 metropolitan boroughs.

TOURIST SITES

Abbey Road

Iconic place where the Beatles recorded the cover of their album "Abbey Road" in 1969. Currently, the zebra crossing, which was the cover of the album, is one more element of traffic, where people and cars cross. Of course, there are almost always a good number of tourists taking photos and cutting traffic to simulate the mythical cover of the group crossing the zebra crossing. Right next door are the famous "Abbey Road Studios" recording studios, where the Beatles recorded several albums and many other famous singers have passed. It is closed to the public and cannot be visited.

Can only be seen from outside. The only thing you can visit is the official store, which is attached to the studios. For fans, I recommend going into the store, it's very good. It's all collectible.

The Sherlock Holmes Museum

Located on Baker Street and opened in 1990 According to the stories of the novelist Sir Arthur Conan Doyle, the Victorian house that occupies the Sherlock Holmes Museum (The Sherlock Holmes Museum), was the home of the main characters of his successful novels, Sherlock Holmes and Dr. Watson, in the company of his housekeeper, Miss Hudson.

We recommend the visit especially to lovers of mystery novels, especially those who grew up with Sherlock Holmes, as it is a unique opportunity to enter the heart of one of their stories.

London Eye

The London Eye Ferris Wheel is centrally located in the heart of the capital, gracefully turning on the River Thames in front of the Houses of Parliament and Big Ben. At 135 meters, the London Eye is the tallest cantilever wheel in the world, a feat of design and engineering that has become the modern symbol representing the capital and a global icon. It offers spectacular 360-degree views of the capital, with its famous landmarks, and has been the most visited tourist attraction in the city for the past decade.

A gradual turn of one of its 32 high-tech capsules takes approximately 30 minutes and offers a changing perspective of London. Inside each capsule, interactive guides allow you to explore the iconic landmarks of the capital in multiple languages.

A ride on the London Eye will lift you high enough to see up to 40 kilometers away on a clear day and close enough to see the spectacular details of the city unfolding before you.

Make the most of your time visiting the London Eye 4D Experience, free with each entry. See how London comes to life through unique perspectives and multi-sensory special effects, including wind, bubbles and scents. The four-minute show is perfect to start the visit.

Shakespeare's Globe Theatre

The quirky Shakespeare's Globe Theater will transport you to 17th century London through the theater world of the time of the illustrious English playwright.

The Globe Theater was originally the company theater owned by the famous English playwright, poet, and actor William Shakespeare. It officially opened in 1599 and had a capacity of more than 3,000 people. Here Shakespeare performed many of his famous plays and became one of the most successful theaters in all of London.

The current theater is in Bankside, just under 200 meters from the old one. It is built according to the Elizabethan plans. It retains the original structure, with a large circular outdoor patio with three rows of seats around the ground floor and boxes on the two upper floors. It is the only thatched-roof London building since it was banned after the Great Fire of 1666.

El Shakespeare's Globe Theatre solo abre sus puertas durante los meses de verano para ver las obras, concretamente desde finales de abril hasta octubre.

Guided tours are carried out throughout the year with the disadvantage that the guides only speak English and it can be somewhat boring for those who are not very familiar with this language. If you are fans of the art of performance, do not miss the staging of some theatrical play in this historic theater that will transport you to the times of Shakespeare.

Charles Dickens Museum

Charles Dickens is one of the authors who are completely associated with the city of London and with whose life a city guide could be drawn. That is why in the Holborn district is his house museum where the writer lived for three years and where Oliver Twist wrote.

Although Dickens lived in several different places in the city and only lived here from 1837 to 1839, in this house he wrote two of his favorite books: Nicholas Nickleby and Oliver Twist

In an already literary neighborhood, Bloomsbury, the house reflects the life of a wealthy family in the Victorian era, it was the early years of his literary career and in the museum.

We can find manuscripts, letters, hundreds of objects belonging to the family and first editions of his novels.

Tower of London

The Tower of London is one of the most steeped monuments in London's history. It is located on the north bank of the River Thames, in the Tower Hamlets district, a strategic location for the defense of the city. In the tower the Crown Jewels are treasured with great protection.

The visit is guided by the Yeoman Warders (the guardians of the Tower of London) Discover its 900 years of history as a royal palace and fortress, prison and place of execution, mint, arsenal, menagerie and jewel house

Westminster Abbey

Westminster Abbey is London's most prominent and historic religious building, where coronations and royal weddings have been held for years. the abbey is a must-see incarnation of British history. The fame of this basilica is not only due to its architectural beauty and decoration, but also due to the events it has accommodated and the personalities who are buried inside it.

The famous people buried here are some of the kings of England and famous figures such as Charles Darwin or Isaac Newton, among many others.Every year the Westminster Abbey welcomes more than a million visitors eager to explore this wonderful building of 700 years, the church of the English coronations. Thousands more people flock to the daily abbey services.

The Shard

The Shard is the tallest skyscraper in the European Union at 310 meters high. In its beginnings it was known as London Bridge Tower. In its 87 floors, 72 of them usable, The Shard has offices (floors 3-28), restaurants (31-33), the luxurious Shangri-La hotel (34-52), apartments (53-65), a gazebo (68-72) and the needle (73-87). The viewpoint of the building, known as "The View", has become next to the London Eye in the best place to observe London from above.

The View is divided into three floors, two of them closed and an open terrace, and the view offered is 360º, making it possible to see up to 65 kilometers around. The height of the gazebo is almost twice that of the London Eye.

Buckingham Palace

Visit Buckingham Palace, one of the few remaining royal palaces in the world. The palace is the Queen's official residence and has hosted many historical events, from royal baptisms to visits by world leaders.

In summer, you can visit the 19 spectacular royal apartments. These sumptuous rooms are decorated with some of the greatest treasures in the royal collection, including paintings by Rembrandt, Rubens, and Canaletto.

Every year, Buckingham Palace hosts a special exhibition as part of the summer opening. The exhibition changes every year, but always offers a fascinating focus on real life.

It is advisable to book tickets for Buckingham Palace in advance. Outside of the summer opening, you can continue visiting the famous palace facade and enjoy the famous changing of the guard.

British Museum

The British Museum is certainly an exceptional museum that holds a bit of the cultural history of the whole world inside. Here you will find pieces from Africa, the Americas, Asia, Europe and the Pacific. Highlights include the Reading Room: one of Karl Marx's most frequented places, the large courtyard designed by Norman Foster, and the mummies on display in the Ancient Egypt Collection.

Entrance to the Museum is free. A donation of £ 5 is recommended. There are admission charges for special exhibitions and some events - book online now to avoid missing out.

Covent Garden Market

Covent Garden is a fascinating commercial and cultural district that provides visitors with a unique experience. The place has a lot to offer, including specialty shops; a wide variety of bars, restaurants and cafes; the Apple Market and shows by street artists. All of this is found in and around the iconic Market Building area and the Piazzas. Covent Garden is a masterpiece of architecture that attracts visitors from all over the world.

It is also possible to spend a pleasant afternoon having a coffee in any of the many venues, visit the London Transport Museum or go to one of the functions of the Royal Opera House. If you can visit the area on a Thursday, the Piazza is filled with stalls of artisan cakes and sweets, and some others that prepare delicious food on the spot.

Hampstead Heath

Hampstead Heath, one of London's most beloved open spaces, is a massive 320-hectare park with centuries-old forests and meadows. It also features an unheated outdoor pool, a zoo, a running track, a multitude of children's facilities, swimming ponds, Kenwood House and Parliament Hill, with one of the best views of London's skyline.

Leicester Square

The cradle of London entertainment is famous for the diversity of its establishments. Catch one of the many shows or watch the dazzling red carpet premieres all in one of the world-class theaters (the average is one release per week). This square attracts actors from Hollywood and from all over the planet, dressed to walk the red carpet. Relax in one of the many restaurants and bars, many offer special events or menus during premieres and before or after the theater.

Trafalgar Square

The central Trafalgar Square is full of life and it is not surprising, as it is one of the most important areas of London. The most characteristic feature of this square built in 1845 is its large column, which pays tribute to Vice Admiral Horatio Nelson, who won the Battle of Trafalgar, which gives its name to the busy square. This column reaches a height of more than 50 meters, crowned by a sculpture of the Vice Admiral.

The obelisk you can see the bronze sculpture of gigantic lions molded by the architect Sir Edwin Lutyens. Flanking the column are the harmonious sources of crystalline water that provide a breath of freshness, especially on hot days.

IWM London: Imperial War Museum London

The Imperial War Museum in London reopened on July 14 after a period of reform. Renovated, it celebrates the centenary of the First World War with new revolutionary galleries of this warlike conflict and a new cloister with large emblematic objects.

Science Museum

The London Science Museum is an interactive museum that brings its visitors closer to science in a fun way, something that makes it the museum of choice for children and adults with an investigative spirit.

Throughout the seven floors of the museum you can find cars, planes, engines and countless inventions that have marked the course of history, in addition to hundreds of interactive experiments with which you can learn how all kinds of things work.

The museum exhibits are divided by areas of knowledge and span countless fields, from the early days of computer science to the evolution of medicine, through the history of space travel and the advancement of telecommunications.

The museum also has some areas (quite a few) that require paid admission, such as the IMAX cinema and a virtual reality simulator.

Natural History Museum

The Natural History Museum in London deals with the Earth and different forms of life. It has a collection made up of more than 70 million specimens and objects related to the natural world. As soon as you enter the museum, the enormous hall with a careful decoration in which some large animals are exposed, such as the skeleton of a huge diplodocus and a mastodon from Chile, is impressive.

The museum is made up of different sections that are exposed throughout the imposing building, among which the following are worth noting:

  1. Dinosaurs: Throughout several rooms you can see dinosaur skeletons, in addition to some life-size dinosaur recreations. It is one of the preferred parts for children.
  2. Mammals: In the biodiversity rooms you can see both mammals (dissected or recreations) and fossils. The center of the ceiling of the main room is decorated with the recreation of an impressive life-size blue whale.
  3. The internal force (The Power Within): This is an interactive experience that aims to show the reasons why volcanoes and earthquakes occur through different recreations.

Emirates Air Line cable car

Ride the Emirates Air Line, London's cable car over the River Thames. Enjoy great views of London as you travel between the Greenwich Peninsula and the Royal Docks.

You can use the Oyster pay as you go card or purchase an Emirates Air Line ticket at the terminals at each end. The adult fare is £ 4.40 one way and £ 10 round trip. If you use the Oyster card it will be 25% cheaper than paying in cash.

The journey is less than 10 minutes one way, and the terminals are close to Metro, DLR, buses and river transport services.

Big Ben

Big Ben is the most representative tower in the city of London and owns one of the largest mechanical clocks in the world. Big Ben is the name by which the tower is known worldwide, but officially this name is due to the main bell, which weighs about 13.8 tons (the first one weighed 16 tons but it broke and was replaced). The construction of Big Ben began in 1843, after the great fire in London on October 16, 1834, which seriously affected the Palace of Westminster. Charles Barry was in charge of the reconstruction project, which added the tower to the palace and designed it in a Victorian Gothic style.

This monument is 96.3 meters high and sits on a 225 square meter base. Each face of Big Ben's watches measures 7.5 meters and the hands are very long; the hour hand is 2.7 meters long while the minute hand is 4.3 meters. The clock numbers are not far behind at 50 centimeters high.

The watch is known for its reliability as it has rarely stopped working. Even after the bombings that London suffered during the Second World War, the watch has not altered its punctual reputation one iota.

Piccadilly Circus

Piccadilly Circus is considered by many to be central London. It is a very busy square with a large influx of both Londoners and tourists of almost all nationalities, moved by the large number of shops and clubs with a lot of nightlife, where to have a beer and spend a pleasant time with friends.

Soho

The Soho neighborhood next to Piccadilly Circus forms the so-called central London. What was once a neighborhood known for its bad reputation is now a multicultural place, full of life and with a special tourist attraction.

Old Compton Street is the main thoroughfare of this picturesque neighborhood and is full of tourists who enjoy its shops, boutiques, restaurants, as well as its popular pubs, discos and some sex-shops.

Another street that stands out in Soho is Leicester Square and its surroundings, full of theaters, cinemas and discotheques where young people have fun on Saturday nights.

City Hall

The City Hall is one of the newest and most modern buildings in London where the offices of the City Council and the City Assemblies are housed. It is located south of the Thames, very close to the Tower Bridge in London.

It is a modern building designed by the well-known architect Norman Foster, the same architect who designed the London Millennium Bridge and who remodeled the iconic Reichstag (German Parliament in Berlin).

The building is about 45 meters high, in which a total of 10 floors are distributed and through which a long spiral staircase of 500 meters in length runs. At the top is an exhibition and meeting room called London's Living Room, with an open gazebo that is sometimes open to the public.

Its modernity is not only applied to its peculiar and futuristic design. This glass and steel building replaces air conditioning with natural ventilation and is precisely inclined to avoid direct sunlight during the most intense hours, so air conditioning is not necessary. It also has a series of solar panels to supply the electrical consumption of the entire building.

Ye Olde Cheshire Cheese

There are many pubs in London that claim that Charles Dickens had a drink there or just frequented them, however, reading the Tale of Two Cities only names one: Ye Olde Cheshire Cheese.

This dark pub is perfectly Dickesian and its ground floor is reminiscent of a cavern or a dungeon, it is not by chance that it dates from 1538. It is also said that Tennyson, Stevenson and Conan Doyle frequently passed by its bar. Despite having more than half a century, this pub is frequented for after office or just to drink a pint on the weekend.

RESTAURANTS

The Sherlock Holmes Pub

The Sherlock Holmes pub is a traditional English pub serving pints and pub food.

As well as a bar and restaurant, the pub has a secret - a complete recreation of Holmes and Watson's study and sitting room with a large collection of objects and photographs related to the characters from the books and adaptations.

The collection was put together for the Festival of Great Britain and moved to this permanent home at the Sherlock Holmes pub in 1957.

The Mayflower

Of all the riverside pubs, this one is the most special. Really really old, really really near the river, really really good. Soak up the atmosphere in a dark, wooden alcove or arrive early for a space on the decked terrace – you’ll have the Thames lapping underneath you when the tide comes in.

Twinings On The Strand

To speak of Twinings is to speak of an icon when it comes to tea. And although you can buy a package of the brand in any supermarket, we encourage you to have a much more genuine experience having a tea in the original Twinings lounge on Strand Street, an authentic place that is considered part of the British Heritage and that it has nothing more and nothing less than ... 300 years of history!

Andy's Taverna

Andy’s Taverna restaurant in a corner of London that could be reasonably nicknamed Little Greek Cyprus is a charming, casual dining spot with all the cheer of the Greek islands. The white-fronted restaurant has classic blue awnings, while a subtler blue and white theme continues indoors to remind you of whitewashed Greek houses and the blue Aegean.

This long-established restaurant is famous for its warm staff, pocket-friendly prices and generous portions. Sea bass and sea bream are flown in daily from Greece. The menu includes your favourite Greek breads and dips to start, main courses of grilled Aegean sea bass with potatoes and salad, and selections of classic Greek pastries for dessert.

Bar 61 Restaurant

Regulars at this well-established south London institution are greeted by name and invited to eat at the bar. Mellow lighting and soft wood tones lend a sense of convivial familiarity to the raised dining area beyond, where families, friends and dates blend comfortably together. Bar 61’s tapas menu features plenty of vegetarian options alongside standards such as chorizo and calamares.

We particularly liked the abundance of chunky vegetables in deftly seasoned ratatouille, and found the meat on our pork cutlets almost tender enough to cut itself. Everything we sampled was a fine match for the bold Bordón crianza that staff recommended from an extensive list of Spanish wines.

Ice Bar

ICE BAR London is one of the most unique and iconic venues in London, where everything, even your glass is made out of ice.

The ICE BAR is located just off Regent Street in the chic Heddon Street Food Quarter, and in addition to the -5°C ICE BAR experience guests can enjoy snacks from Yuu Kitchen and unusual and delicious cocktails from Fire Bar.

Belowzero is the UK’s only permanent ice bar, keeping things a cool -5℃ all year round. To keep things fresh, they redesign the space each year, putting their team of artists to work to create an entirely new theme. This year, they’ve dedicated the bar to all things London, so get ready to see the world’s greatest city in a whole new light, with loads of cheeky nods to London life, including references to Banksy, Brexit, and Her Majesty The Queen.

Café OZ

The Oz is located at 53 Caledonian Road, King's Cross, and is a good place if you are out for a walk in the area for a full English breakfast or a family lunch. It is worth mentioning that children are very welcome and that attention is unquestionable.

Beigel shop

This place is one of the most famous beige bakeries in the city. The bakery is located at 159 Brick Lane and is open 24 hours a day. Here you can buy the best beigels to take away, cooked in the traditional Jewish style, filled with salted veal with mustard, herring, salmon ... among many other ingredients.

You can also buy dessert since they serve various cakes and sweets. At meal times, you will see a long line form outside, but don't panic, they serve fairly quickly and it won't take long for you to get your beigel.

There is an exiting concept with new pop-up restaurants at the rooftop every now and then, however always with an Italian base. The 2020 season will see the roof turn into Serra, an Italian countryside restaurant, available also during the colder months as Serra Alpina.

Cafe Boheme

Cafe Boheme is a classic among the classics of the city of the Thames. It is a famous French bistro with more than 25 years in the heart of Soho. What makes it different? In addition to its decoration and its varied menu, the closure of its kitchen.

And it is that, its stoves continue working until 3 in the morning making it one of the great centers of London's nightlife.

ITINERARY

London is such a big city, it is recommended that you travel to it knowing in advance what you are going to do and what places have priority for you. In this way, you will not go without visiting those places of obligatory visit.

A good way to organize a trip is planning daily itineraries or routes to know which places to visit at all times and thus, not waste time going from one place to another.

CENTRAL LONDON

A good way to start off on the right foot may be to travel to the Queensway Station on the Red Line and begin your visit to Kensington Palace and its gardens. From there you can go down, enjoying a walk through Hyde Park and Green Park. Here we recommend you visit the Buckingham Palace. Without a doubt this whole tour will take you a good time, especially if you want to do it well, so it is a good time to have lunch. A quick meal in Green Park can be an excellent alternative, you can also opt for a delicious lunch at the Borough market.

In the afternoon you can head to Big Ben to admire this iconic building and also visit Westminster Abbey and the Palace of Westminster, which are two unmissable sites in London. Then head to Palace-Westminster, and descend to London Bridge. If you want to enjoy good views of the city go to the London Eye. You can spend the rest of the afternoon walking around, enjoying the postcards that form along the Thames at sunset and enjoying a beer in a pub on the promenade.

THE MOST IMPORTANT MUSEUMS

The second day in London is dedicated to getting to know some of the most representative museums in the history of the city. First, visit the British Museum, located near Russell Square. This visit will take a long time because it is a very big place and full of interesting things, so it is recommended that you spend all morning.

Then you can have a quick lunch in the area and then head to Piccadilly Circus station and visit the National Gallery, the Institute of Contemporary Art and the Charles Dickens Museum. There are many interesting places in the area, if you are interested in reading.

other museums you can visit without Museum of London, National Gallery, Natural History Museum, Science Museum, Freud Museum, Cartoon Museum, The Crime Museum, Sherlock Holmes Museum.

COVENT GARDEN, THE SOHO AND CHINATOWN

One of the best ways to start your day is with a full English breakfast! For that you can go to one of the bars that are near your hotel and opt for the one that inspires you the most.

After this delicious breakfast, head to the Covent Garden area, where you can visit one of the most exquisite neighborhoods in the city. Then head to Seven Dials, which is a corner of London that is sure to catch your eye and keep walking towards Shaftesbury Ave, on a colorful walk through a different area of ​​London. You can take advantage to eat something in the area.

In the afternoon, visit Chinatown and Soho, a fascinating and varied neighborhood in the heart of London, where you can enjoy very interesting shops and a curious atmosphere. To end this wonderful day we recommend you go to Piccadilly Circus to see the impressive neon signs that have made this city so popular and have a beer in some English pub.

EXCURSION TO STONEHENGE AND BATH

Visiting these two iconic places in the UK is a great recommendation, that is if you bring snacks to enjoy a different journey.

Stonehenge (it is advisable to wear a lot of coat because the weather is much colder than London). To tell the truth, the ideal would be to dedicate a whole day to this place, to explore the surroundings in detail. You should know that the stone circle cannot be visited from very close; You must be content to see it at a certain distance, but if you do the guided tour you will be able to find out in detail about its history and it is worth it.

Bath, a very significant place where an impressive Roman bath is preserved that attracts thousands of tourists every year. In this city you can visit some very literary places, related to the world of Jane Austen. A good idea may be to spend the night in Bath and enjoy a night out quite different from what London offers you.

EXCURSION TO OXFORD AND WARWICK

This is the excursion to Oxford and Warwick, you can discover ancient buildings and a colorful British region.

The trip to Oxford will take about two hours, among the things you can visit are the County Hall (a town hall with a medieval style that you will fall in love with right away), the Cathedral of Christ Church and the University of Oxford itself. Other interesting sites are the Radcliffe Camera, St. Mary the Virgin Church and the Carfax Tower. But if you are passionate about nature, what you should not miss is the Natural History Museum.

In Warwick you can enjoy a very interesting castle that reflects a good part of British history. Also don't miss a stroll through Charlecote Park, St. Nicholas Park and a visit to the Warwikshire Museum.

Before the end of the day you can return to London and enjoy a delicious dinner in a nice restaurant to say goodbye to London.
